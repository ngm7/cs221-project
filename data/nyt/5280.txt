When Lora Safir was 5, her father abruptly returned to their home in Odessa from the Ukrainian battleground of World War II.

He had been fighting as a soldier in the Red Army and heard that the Romanian army would occupy Odessa on Hitler’s command later that day.

“He told us that we had 10 minutes to get out,” Mrs. Safir, 82, recalled in an interview this month. “We barely managed to get our passports, a blanket and a pillow.”

Mrs. Safir, her mother, father and two brothers climbed on top of a cargo truck, but some of their aunts, uncles and young cousins chose to stay.
